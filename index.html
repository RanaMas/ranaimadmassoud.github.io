Bio

I am a PhD candidate in computational physical chemistry specializing in molecular dynamics simulations, enhanced sampling methods, and structure-based modeling of small molecule–protein interactions. My work sits at the interface of computational chemistry, molecular modeling, and biological systems analysis.

My research has evolved toward computational toxicology and environmental health, where I apply molecular simulation and modeling approaches to understand how environmental chemicals interact with biological targets and contribute to adverse biological outcomes. A major focus of my work is on per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS), where I investigate their interactions with nuclear receptors and other biologically relevant proteins to better understand their potential mechanisms of toxicity, persistence, and bioactivity in environmental systems (invertebrates, vertebrates and mammals).

In parallel, I have contributed to structure-based drug discovery projects targeting Mycobacterium tuberculosis MmpL3 transporter, GPCRs and Ddns. These projects involve molecular docking, pharmacophore modeling, fragment-based modeling and molecular dynamics simulations.

Beyond human health and pharmacological applications, my main interest extends into environmental modeling frameworks, investigating how chemical structure influences biological interaction profiles across different receptors and species. This includes modeling endocrine disruption pathways in aquatic organisms and evaluating the potential impact of industrial and environmental chemicals using in silico approaches. My work bridges atomistic-level understanding to broader questions in environmental risk assessment, chemical safety, and regulatory-relevant decision-making.

I am particularly interested in New Approach Methodologies (NAMs) that reduce reliance on experimental testing while improving predictive understanding of chemical hazards.
